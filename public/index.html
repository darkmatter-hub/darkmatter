Why it exists

Every commercial flight has a black box. Every AI agent should too.

Aviation didn't put flight data recorders in planes because pilots were untrustworthy. It put them there because when something goes wrong at altitude, memory is unreliable, incentives are misaligned, and the only thing that matters is what actually happened.

The aviation parallel

The flight data recorder is independent by design. It doesn't live inside the cockpit systems. It doesn't report to the airline. It survives the crash and tells investigators the truth.

Nobody argues the recorder should be optional. Nobody argues the airline should keep its own records and report honestly. The independence is the point. Without it, every investigation starts with the question: who do you trust?

AI agents are making consequential decisions right now — approving payments, flagging contracts, routing customers, generating outputs that reach real people. When something goes wrong, the record of what happened lives inside the same system that made the decision. That system is controlled by the same team that needs the investigation to go well.

That's not a black box. That's a system vouching for itself.

DarkMatter is the flight data recorder for AI systems. Every agent action recorded outside the system that produced it, sealed at the moment it happens, and reconstructable by anyone who needs to know what actually occurred.

When something goes wrong

"Your agent just approved an $84,000 payment to a vendor you've never heard of. It happened in 340 milliseconds. Nobody was watching. What do you do next?"

If your logs live inside your own infrastructure, you're starting from a position of defending yourself with evidence you control. A regulator, an auditor, a counterparty — they have every reason to question it. Evidence you produced is the weakest kind of evidence.

If the record lives in DarkMatter, the answer is a URL. You send it to anyone who needs it. They open it. The chain is there — every step, every input, every output, sealed at the moment it happened. They verify it themselves without asking you to vouch for anything.

How it works

Record now. Reconstruct later. Verify without asking anyone.

DarkMatter does three things. It doesn't run your agents, manage your prompts, or judge whether the output was good. It records, replays, and proves.

dm.commit()

Record every decision outside your system

Every agent action becomes an immutable node — input, output, model, tool calls, timestamp — chained to the action before it, written outside your infrastructure the moment it happens. Not a log. A sealed record.

dm.replay() / dm.fork()

Reconstruct exactly what happened

Walk the full decision chain step by step, root to tip. Branch from any checkpoint to investigate what would have happened differently. The original is always intact. Nothing is overwritten.

dm.export() / dm.verify()

Prove it without asking anyone to trust you

Export a self-contained proof bundle with an embedded standalone verifier. No DarkMatter account, no internet connection, no asking you to vouch. Anyone can run it. The record proves itself.

Verification levels

Not every record carries the same weight. DarkMatter makes that explicit.

Every commit starts at Level 1. Move to Level 2 or 3 as your compliance requirements grow. The difference is visible in every record and verifiable independently.

Level What it means How you get it
Verifiable record SHA-256 payload hash + parent chain linkage. Tampering at any node breaks every downstream hash. Independent offline verifier included. Default with every dm.commit() call. No extra steps.
Independent verification Signed checkpoint bundles anchored to Bitcoin via OpenTimestamps. Timestamp proof lives outside DarkMatter's infrastructure entirely. Automatic. Every checkpoint batch is anchored and signed.
Non-repudiation Customer-held signing keys. Even DarkMatter cannot forge a record — only your key can sign a valid commit. Verification works without revealing the payload. SDK with your own Ed25519 key (BYOK). Roadmap.
